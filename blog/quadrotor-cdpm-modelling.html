Modelling & Simulation of a
Quadrotor-Based Cable-Driven
Parallel Manipulator

How we built a full dynamic simulation framework coupling aerial robots with cables to create a new class of reconfigurable manipulation system — and what we learned along the way.

Siddharth Umakarthikeyan
Ph.D. Researcher · SASTRA University
Published at IEEE ICCMC 2023 8 min read
Cable-Driven Manipulators Quadrotor ROS Gazebo Dynamics Ph.D. Research

The Problem: Workspace is Everything

Cable-Driven Parallel Manipulators (CDPMs) are fascinating machines. Instead of rigid links, they use cables to position an end-effector — making them inherently lightweight, fast, and capable of spanning enormous workspaces. You see them in sports broadcasting (the flying cameras over stadiums), in large-scale 3D printing, and in rehabilitation robotics.

But they have a hard constraint: cables can only pull, never push. This means the end-effector's reachable workspace — the set of poses where all cable tensions remain positive — is severely limited by where you anchor the cables. Fix your anchor points to a ceiling or a frame, and you're stuck with that geometry forever.

What if the anchor points themselves could move? What if they were drones?

That was the core question driving this research. By replacing fixed anchor points with quadrotors, we could — in theory — dynamically reconfigure the entire manipulator geometry on the fly. The wrench-feasible workspace would no longer be a fixed region; it would become a function of where we position the drones.

System Architecture

The system we modelled consists of n quadrotors, each carrying one end of a cable attached to a common rigid end-effector platform. The quadrotors act simultaneously as:

  • Anchor points — providing the attachment geometry for the cable system
  • Active actuators — generating thrust forces transmitted through the cables
  • Mobile agents — repositioning themselves to reconfigure the manipulator workspace

This dual role creates a deeply coupled system. A change in quadrotor position changes cable direction vectors, which changes the wrench matrix, which changes what forces and torques the system can apply to the end-effector. Nothing is independent.

Dynamic Modelling

The full dynamic model captures three coupled subsystems:

  1. Quadrotor dynamics — rigid body 6-DOF model with rotor thrust and drag torques, using Newton-Euler formulation
  2. Cable model — massless inextensible cables with unilateral tension constraints (tension ≥ 0)
  3. End-effector dynamics — rigid body acted on by the resultant cable wrenches and gravity

The wrench matrix W maps cable tensions to end-effector wrench (force + torque). For n cables attached at known directions: 

W = [u₁  u₂  ...  uₙ  ]   ← force rows
    [r₁×u₁  r₂×u₂  ...  rₙ×uₙ]   ← torque rows

τ = W · t,   t ≥ 0   (tension constraints)

Where uᵢ is the unit cable direction vector from end-effector to drone i, and rᵢ is the moment arm. The wrench-feasible workspace is the set of end-effector poses where a valid tension vector exists for any external wrench within given bounds.

Wrench-Feasible Workspace Analysis

We computed the WFW numerically by discretising the end-effector translation space into a 20×20×20 grid (fixed orientation), then solving the tension-allocation QP at each cell — flagging the cell as feasible if a valid non-negative tension vector exists. For a symmetric 4-drone square formation at 2 m altitude with 1 m cable length, the WFW occupies roughly 18% of the bounding box.

Two straightforward geometry changes change this significantly: extending cable length to 1.5 m expands the WFW to 31% of the bounding box, and adding a fifth drone off-centre expands it to 44% while also closing directional gaps in the 4-drone case. The actionable design conclusion: cable length and formation shape are the dominant design levers — not drone thrust capacity. Optimise geometry first.

Simulation in Gazebo + ROS

We implemented the full simulation in Gazebo with ROS as the middleware. Each quadrotor runs an independent PID attitude controller. The cable dynamics are approximated using Gazebo's joint constraints with one-directional force limits — a practical compromise that preserves the fundamental unilateral constraint while remaining real-time capable.

The ROS architecture uses a central trajectory planner node that broadcasts desired end-effector poses and required cable tensions, with individual quadrotor controllers tracking their assigned positions to maintain the geometry.

Controller Architecture

The control hierarchy has three levels. At the top, the end-effector trajectory planner publishes desired poses at 10 Hz. The middle layer is the cable tension allocator: given the desired end-effector wrench and current drone positions it solves a constrained QP to find the minimum-norm cable tension vector satisfying W·t = τ_des with t ≥ 0. At the bottom, each drone runs a cascade PD controller — an outer position loop (10 Hz) computing desired thrust and attitude, feeding into a 100 Hz attitude inner loop: 

/* Attitude inner loop — 100 Hz */
τ_roll  = Kp_φ·(φ_d − φ) + Kd_φ·(φ̇_d − φ̇)
τ_pitch = Kp_θ·(θ_d − θ) + Kd_θ·(θ̇_d − θ̇)
τ_yaw   = Kp_ψ·(ψ_d − ψ) + Kd_ψ·(ψ̇_d − ψ̇)

/* Motor mixing — X-frame quadrotor */
[ω₁²]   [ 1   1   1   1 ] [F_total / 4kT ]
[ω₂²] = [-1   1  -1   1 ]·[τ_roll  / 4kTl]
[ω₃²]   [-1  -1   1   1 ] [τ_pitch / 4kTl]
[ω₄²]   [ 1  -1  -1   1 ] [τ_yaw   / 4kD ]

The critical issue: the tension allocator assumes drones are exactly at their desired positions when computing cable direction vectors. In practice, drones track position setpoints with 80–120 ms of lag at typical operating speeds. This creates a geometric mismatch between the model and reality — appearing as an unmodelled disturbance wrench at the end-effector. For four drones these errors can constructively add or partially cancel depending on formation symmetry, making symmetric formations simultaneously harder to stabilise and easier to analyse.

Key finding: The dynamic coupling between quadrotor flight controllers and cable tension control is the dominant source of instability. Independent PID controllers for each drone, without awareness of the cable system, cause oscillation at the end-effector even when individual drones track their setpoints accurately.

What the Simulation Revealed

Running systematic simulations across different quadrotor configurations gave us several non-obvious insights:

  • Geometry dominates everything. The shape of the convex hull formed by the drone positions determines the wrench-feasible workspace far more than thrust capacity. A poorly arranged formation of high-power drones underperforms a well-arranged formation of modest ones.
  • Symmetric configurations are not always optimal. For asymmetric payloads or directional tasks, deliberately asymmetric drone placement increases the workspace volume in the direction of interest at the cost of reducing it elsewhere — a useful design trade-off.
  • Cable sag matters at scale. At prototype scale (sub-2m spans), the massless cable approximation holds well. As span increases beyond ~5m, cable mass and catenary effects begin to significantly distort the tension estimates.
  • Inter-drone communication latency is a hard constraint. At ≥50ms latency between the central planner and drone controllers, the coupled system becomes marginally stable at typical end-effector load conditions.
  • Coupled tension-aware control dramatically outperforms independent PIDs. Simulating independent per-drone PIDs (no awareness of cable coupling) versus a tension-aware coupled controller showed a 60–70% reduction in steady-state end-effector position error. The gain comes from preventing opposing drones from fighting each other's control actions — a failure mode that is invisible to each individual drone controller in isolation.

Why This Matters

The immediate value is a validated simulation framework — a digital twin of the system that lets us test trajectories, controller designs, and drone configurations before committing to hardware. That matters enormously for a system where a bad cable tension command can crash four drones simultaneously.

More broadly, the quadrotor-CDPM concept opens up genuinely new application spaces: construction and inspection tasks at height where scaffold-free large-workspace manipulation is valuable; disaster response where a reconfigurable aerial manipulation system can be rapidly deployed; and spacecraft assembly analogues in microgravity environments.

This paper lays the dynamic foundation. The follow-on work — expanding the wrench-feasible workspace using multi-objective optimisation and machine learning (published in 2024) — builds directly on the models developed here.
