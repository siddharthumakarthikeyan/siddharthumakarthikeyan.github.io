Intelligent Path Planning for Tow-Based AGVs in Dynamic Manufacturing Environments

How we built a hybrid Dynamic Programming–LiDAR–TEB navigation stack that outperforms A*, Dijkstra and TEB planners — and validated it from simulation all the way to real hardware.

Flexible manufacturing plants run on logistics. Parts move from storage to machining stations, assemblies travel between production cells, and finished components queue for dispatch — constantly, without supervision. When that material flow depends on autonomous vehicles, the navigation problem is deceptively hard: not because the environment is complex in isolation, but because it changes in ways no static planner anticipates.

The Vehicle — and What Makes It Hard

We built and tested this on a differential-drive tow-based AGV pulling a cart on an off-axis hitch, with four freely-swivelling caster wheels on the trailer. Tug-and-cart configurations are extremely common in real plants because they are mechanically simple and cheap to maintain. But the trailer changes the navigation problem in ways most path-planning research ignores. As the AGV turns, each caster pivots at its own rate. Take a corner too sharply and the trailer doesn't follow the robot body cleanly — it swings wide, encroaches on the production line on either side, or jackknifes at the coupling.

"A path that looks perfectly smooth for the robot can still whip the trailer into the machinery beside it."

The Operating Constraints

The factory floor isn't a clean warehouse aisle. It's a 15 × 15 metre space where production workers move between workstations, other vehicles park and depart at unpredictable times, and the robot must stay inside a defined operational corridor at all times. Leaving that corridor — even briefly, even to dodge an obstacle — counts as a failure. Production lines run right up to the boundary on both sides.

Existing planners struggle with this hard. A* and Dijkstra search for the shortest path in all available free space. They don't know what a "corridor" means — they'll route straight through a forbidden zone the moment an obstacle makes it locally optimal. TEB, the elastic-band planner widely used in ROS, generates smooth curves but provides no guarantee about staying inside a boundary. We tested all three. Every one exited the operating corridor under obstacle pressure at some point.

A Three-Layer Navigation Architecture

Rather than using a single planner, we separated navigation into three layers, each handling a distinct sub-problem at a different time horizon and spatial scale.

At the top sits a strategic question: given a goal across the factory floor, which sequence of via-points should the robot follow? This only needs to run when something changes — a blocked route, a new task. We solved it with Dynamic Programming across a graph of predefined checkpoints scattered through the floor. The algorithm finds the optimal sequence in milliseconds. Crucially, arriving at each checkpoint resets the localisation uncertainty — preventing the position drift that accumulates silently over long LiDAR-only runs.

In the middle sits a situational question: what's around the robot right now, and is it a threat? A single RPLidar A1M8 — affordable, illumination-independent, 360° coverage at 12 m range — provides everything. We track each scan return over time. Returns that stay fixed are static obstacles: a pallet left in the aisle, a machine rolled out for maintenance. Returns that move are dynamic: a human walking through. For a moving obstacle, the robot doesn't replan — it smoothly reduces speed, gives the person time to clear, then continues. No jerky stops. No abrupt swerves that would stress the trailer coupling.

At the bottom sits an execution question: how do you actually drive from checkpoint to checkpoint while keeping the trailer inside the corridor? A modified TEB planner handles this, its cost function tuned to penalise proximity to the corridor boundary heavily. An MPC controller converts the planned path into motor commands, taking real-time trailer feedback from AprilTag markers detected by a rear-facing camera — giving the controller the hitch angle it needs to prevent jackknifing through tight turns.

Simulation Results

We ran all four planners — A*, Dijkstra, TEB alone, and the proposed approach — through the same scenario: a 15 × 15 m CoppeliaSim environment with 11 checkpoints, static machinery obstacles, and human avatars crossing the AGV routes at random. The task: navigate reliably from A to I, staying inside the defined corridor throughout.

A* and Dijkstra both produced paths with visible kinks — moments where replanning caused abrupt direction reversals. For a solo robot that's manageable. For a tow vehicle it's dangerous: the trailer overshoots the turn and swings toward the corridor boundary. In several runs it crossed it entirely. Dijkstra was worse in longer missions, producing path configurations where replanning looped the trajectory back on itself — a condition where trailer geometry becomes irrecoverable without operator intervention.

TEB did much better on smoothness but struggled whenever a dynamic obstacle forced its elastic band to deform. It deformed in whatever direction reduced energy — often directly into the virtual boundary.

The proposed planner didn't do any of that. When an obstacle blocked the route between G and H entirely, it quietly rerouted through K, L, and M — staying inside the corridor the whole time. When a human walked toward it, it slowed, waited, and continued. No boundary violations. No jackknifing.

We also ran 30 trials each with randomised obstacle positions and human trajectories — specifically to check whether those headline numbers were a fluke. They weren't. The proposed planner completed missions in 38.2 ± 1.9 seconds on average. TEB managed 46.7 ± 4.8. A* was 58.1 ± 6.1. The variance tells the deeper story: in a safety-critical environment, an unpredictable planner is often more dangerous than a slow one.

"Unpredictable behaviour is often more dangerous than slow behaviour."

We also scaled up to 10 AGVs running simultaneously on the same floor. DP computation climbed from 12 ms to 17 ms — still well inside real-time limits. Boundary violations remained zero across every fleet size tested.

Hardware Validation

Simulation results need to be confirmed on real hardware. We built the actual AGV with trailer, set up a physical test environment matching the CoppeliaSim layout, and ran all four planners on the real robot. Onboard: Intel NUC i5 for computation, a single RPLidar A1M8 for all obstacle sensing, and a rear-facing camera reading AprilTag markers on the trailer to give the controller live hitch angle data.

The hardware results matched the simulation closely — and in some respects were more revealing. The caster dynamics that the simulator approximated analytically were fully physical here, and they amplified every abrupt direction change in A* and Dijkstra paths visibly. The proposed planner's trajectories were smooth enough that the trailer followed predictably through every run. When we deliberately blocked the G→H segment mid-mission, the system rerouted to G→K→L→M→H without operator input.

Key Takeaways

There are more powerful approaches to AGV navigation — deep reinforcement learning, multi-sensor fusion, centimetre-level SLAM. This work isn't competing with those. It's designed around the practical constraints of real plant deployment: one sensor, a standard single-board computer, and a hard requirement that the vehicle never drifts outside its designated corridor.

What makes it work is the separation of concerns. Strategic replanning doesn't happen fifty times a second — it happens when the situation actually changes. Human avoidance doesn't require a full replan — it requires a speed adjustment. Local execution doesn't need to solve the global problem — it just needs to follow the checkpoints cleanly. Put those three things together and you get a system that is faster than A*, smoother than TEB, and safer than both. With a trailer attached.

One honest caveat: at higher speeds, the trailer boundary guarantee becomes harder to maintain. The caster swivel dynamics need time to settle after a turn, and at speed that time isn't there. We're still mapping the threshold above which the guarantee breaks down.
